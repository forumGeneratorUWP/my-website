Honey bees (Apis mellifera) are indispensable pollinators, contributing to the reproduction of numerous plant species and the production of various crops. Their activities support biodiversity and are crucial for global food security.

Threats to Honey Bee Populations

These factors often interact synergistically, exacerbating their individual impacts and leading to phenomena like Colony Collapse Disorder (CCD), where worker bees abruptly disappear from hives.

Honey bees (Apis mellifera) are among the most important pollinators in the world. Their contribution to both natural ecosystems and agriculture cannot be overstated. Approximately 75% of the world’s leading food crops depend, at least in part, on animal pollination — with honey bees being the dominant contributors among pollinators. They assist in the production of fruits, vegetables, nuts, seeds, and oils, significantly influencing human diets and food availability.

In wild ecosystems, bees help maintain biodiversity by ensuring the reproduction of flowering plants, which in turn supports various herbivores, predators, and decomposers. This intricate web of life depends heavily on successful pollination. A decline in bee populations can therefore have cascading effects on ecosystem stability and resilience, leading to diminished plant diversity and altered habitats for numerous animal species.

Additionally, honey bees have evolved complex communication systems, such as the waggle dance, which allow them to efficiently share information about the location of nectar and pollen sources. This behavior enhances their ability to pollinate large areas effectively, making them irreplaceable in many large-scale agricultural systems. In some regions, farmers rent beehives during flowering seasons specifically to increase crop yields.
